Electromagnetism

From static charges to light itself, electromagnetism unites electricity and magnetism into a single field theory governed by Maxwell’s equations.

Maxwell’s Equations

James Clerk Maxwell condensed the experimental laws of Coulomb, Gauss, Faraday, and Ampère into four equations. In differential form: \[ \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}, \quad \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0, \] \[ \nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}, \quad \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}. \] These equations describe how charges and currents create fields and how those fields evolve in space and time.

Integral Form

The integral forms highlight global relationships: \[ \oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{encl}}}{\varepsilon_0}, \quad \oint \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{A} = 0, \] \[ \oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{l} = -\frac{d}{dt}\Phi_B, \quad \oint \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{encl}} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{d}{dt}\Phi_E. \] Gauss’s law links electric flux to charge, Faraday’s law connects changing magnetic flux to induced emf, and Ampère–Maxwell’s law shows how currents and displacement current generate magnetic fields.

Physical Meaning

Together, Maxwell’s equations mean no isolated magnetic charges exist; changing magnetic fields create electric fields; changing electric fields create magnetic fields; and charges are the source of electric fields. These four relations unify electric and magnetic phenomena into one framework, revealing their deep interdependence.

Lorentz Force

The link between fields and matter is the Lorentz force: \[ \vec{F} = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v}\times \vec{B}). \] It explains why charges accelerate in electric fields, why current-carrying wires deflect in magnetic fields, and why cosmic rays spiral along magnetic field lines. This equation, together with Maxwell’s equations, completes classical electrodynamics.

Potentials and Gauge Freedom

Fields can be expressed in terms of potentials: \[ \vec{E} = -\nabla \phi - \frac{\partial \vec{A}}{\partial t}, \quad \vec{B} = \nabla \times \vec{A}. \] Potentials are not unique: adding a gauge transformation \(\chi\) leaves \(\vec{E}\) and \(\vec{B}\) unchanged. This gauge freedom foreshadows modern quantum field theory, where symmetries of potentials give rise to fundamental forces.

Boundary Conditions

At material boundaries, fields obey continuity conditions: \[ \hat{n}\cdot(\vec{D}_2-\vec{D}_1)=\sigma_f, \quad \hat{n}\cdot(\vec{B}_2-\vec{B}_1)=0, \] \[ \hat{n}\times(\vec{E}_2-\vec{E}_1)=0, \quad \hat{n}\times(\vec{H}_2-\vec{H}_1)=\vec{K}_f. \] These determine reflection and refraction of waves at interfaces, forming the basis of optics, dielectric design, and microwave engineering.

Electromagnetic Waves

Maxwell’s equations predict wave solutions with speed \[ c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}}. \] These are transverse: \(\vec{E}\perp \vec{B}\perp \vec{k}\). The electromagnetic spectrum spans radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays, all governed by the same equations.

Polarization

Polarization describes the orientation of the electric field vector. Linear polarization aligns the field along one axis; circular polarization arises when two perpendicular components are phase-shifted; elliptical polarization is the general case. Polarization is exploited in antennas, sunglasses, quantum communication, and tests of fundamental physics.

Energy and Momentum

Energy density in fields: \[ u=\tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2+\tfrac{1}{2\mu_0}B^2. \] Energy flux is given by the Poynting vector: \[ \vec{S}=\frac{1}{\mu_0}\vec{E}\times \vec{B}. \] Momentum density is \(\vec{S}/c^2\). These explain light pressure, solar sails, and momentum exchange in radiation.

Radiation and Antennas

Accelerating charges emit radiation. A dipole antenna produces radiation with intensity pattern \(\sin^2\theta\). The Larmor formula gives radiated power: \[ P=\frac{\mu_0 q^2 a^2}{6\pi c}. \] This principle powers radio, radar, and mobile communication. Synchrotron radiation from high-speed charges explains astrophysical emissions from pulsars and galaxies.

Multipole Expansion

Radiation fields can be analyzed using multipole expansion: dipole, quadrupole, octupole, and beyond. Each term corresponds to a pattern of source distribution. Dipole dominates at long wavelengths; higher multipoles explain fine features of atomic transitions and gravitational radiation analogies.

Relativity and Unification

Special relativity unifies electric and magnetic fields into the electromagnetic tensor \(F_{\mu\nu}\). A purely electric field in one frame may appear as a mixture of electric and magnetic fields in another. Maxwell’s equations are Lorentz covariant, embedding light’s constancy into spacetime geometry.

Modern Significance

Electromagnetism powers modern civilization: power grids, electronics, fiber optics, lasers, MRI, satellites, and wireless communication. At a deeper level, quantum electrodynamics refines Maxwell’s equations with quantum corrections, becoming one of the most precise physical theories ever tested.
