Electromagnetism

The field that unites electricity, magnetism, and light — from static charges to waves that fill the cosmos.

Maxwell’s Equations

Maxwell synthesized experimental laws into four compact field equations. In differential form: \[ \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}, \quad \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0, \] \[ \nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}, \quad \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0\vec{J} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}. \] They link charges, currents, and time-varying fields into one coherent theory.

Physical Meaning

Each equation carries deep insight: Gauss’s law ties charge to flux; Gauss’s law for magnetism forbids monopoles; Faraday’s law explains induction; Ampère–Maxwell’s law predicts displacement current. Taken together, they unify electricity and magnetism into one field description.

Lorentz Force

Fields influence charges through the Lorentz force: \[ \vec{F}=q(\vec{E}+\vec{v}\times \vec{B}). \] It explains how wires deflect in magnetic fields, why particles spiral in accelerators, and how cosmic rays paint auroras in Earth’s sky.

Potentials and Gauge Freedom

Fields can be expressed in terms of potentials: \[ \vec{E}=-\nabla \phi - \frac{\partial \vec{A}}{\partial t}, \quad \vec{B}=\nabla\times \vec{A}. \] Gauge freedom allows transformations of \(\phi\) and \(\vec{A}\) without altering physical fields, a symmetry that later became central to modern field theories.

Boundary Conditions

At material boundaries, Maxwell’s equations impose continuity conditions. The normal component of electric flux jumps by surface charge, the tangential electric field is continuous, the normal component of magnetic flux is continuous, and the tangential magnetic field jumps with surface current. These rules explain reflection, refraction, and waveguides.

Electromagnetic Waves

From Maxwell’s equations emerges the wave equation with speed \[ c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}}. \] Electric and magnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to each other and the direction of travel. The entire electromagnetic spectrum — from radio to gamma rays — follows from this result.

Polarization

Polarization describes the orientation of the electric field. Linear polarization fixes a direction; circular polarization rotates the field at light speed; elliptical polarization generalizes both. Applications range from sunglasses to quantum key distribution.

Energy and Momentum

The fields store and transmit energy. Energy density is \[ u=\tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2+\tfrac{1}{2\mu_0}B^2, \] and the Poynting vector \[ \vec{S}=\frac{1}{\mu_0}\vec{E}\times \vec{B} \] gives energy flux. Radiation pressure and solar sails are direct consequences.

Radiation and Antennas

Accelerating charges radiate. Dipole antennas emit with \(\sin^2\theta\) patterns. The Larmor formula, \[ P=\frac{\mu_0 q^2 a^2}{6\pi c}, \] gives radiated power. This principle enables radio, radar, and astrophysical signals.

Multipole Expansion

Radiation can be decomposed into multipoles: dipole dominates at long wavelengths, quadrupole and higher reveal subtle angular structures. This hierarchy explains atomic transitions, molecular spectra, and even analogies to gravitational waves.

Relativity and Unification

Special relativity unifies \(\vec{E}\) and \(\vec{B}\) as components of the electromagnetic tensor \(F_{\mu\nu}\). Fields transform into one another between observers. Maxwell’s equations are naturally Lorentz covariant, embedding the constancy of light speed into spacetime structure.

Modern Significance

Electromagnetism governs modern civilization: power grids, electronics, fiber optics, lasers, satellites, and MRI. The quantum version, QED, refines Maxwell’s theory and is the most precise theory ever tested. Yet the classical equations remain indispensable, elegant, and universal.
