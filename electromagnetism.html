Electromagnetism

An exploration of Maxwell’s framework for electric and magnetic fields — from static charges to light as a self-propagating wave.

Maxwell’s Equations

Electromagnetism rests on four equations that tie charges, currents, and fields into a single theory. In differential form: \[ \nabla\!\cdot\!\vec{E}=\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}, \qquad \nabla\!\cdot\!\vec{B}=0, \] \[ \nabla\!\times\!\vec{E}=-\,\frac{\partial\vec{B}}{\partial t}, \qquad \nabla\!\times\!\vec{B}=\mu_0\vec{J}+\mu_0\varepsilon_0\,\frac{\partial\vec{E}}{\partial t}. \] Gauss’s law links charge to electric flux; the absence of magnetic monopoles forces magnetic field lines to form loops; Faraday’s law explains induction; and the Ampère–Maxwell law shows that both currents and changing electric fields generate magnetism.

The integral statements emphasize global structure: \[ \oiint\vec{E}\!\cdot\!d\vec{A}=\frac{Q_{\rm encl}}{\varepsilon_0}, \quad \oiint\vec{B}\!\cdot\!d\vec{A}=0, \] \[ \oint\vec{E}\!\cdot\!d\vec{l}=-\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}, \quad \oint\vec{B}\!\cdot\!d\vec{l}=\mu_0 I_{\rm encl}+\mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}. \] These forms power practical calculations for capacitors, inductors, transformers, and waveguides.

Fields, Sources, and Matter

Electric fields \(\vec{E}\) act on charge; magnetic fields \(\vec{B}\) act on moving charge. The Lorentz force, \[ \vec{F}=q\big(\vec{E}+\vec{v}\times\vec{B}\big), \] connects fields to motion, explaining drift in plasmas, beam steering in accelerators, and the helical paths of cosmic rays. Superposition makes analysis tractable: fields from many sources simply add.

Materials shape fields. Conductors force \(\vec{E}\) to be tangential at the surface; insulators support polarization; ferromagnets concentrate \(\vec{B}\). At boundaries, normal \(\vec{D}\) jumps by free surface charge, tangential \(\vec{E}\) is continuous, normal \(\vec{B}\) is continuous, and tangential \(\vec{H}\) jumps by surface current — rules that explain reflection, refraction, and impedance matching.

Energy and Work in Fields

Fields store energy with density \(\displaystyle u=\tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2+\tfrac{1}{2\mu_0}B^2\). The Poynting vector, \(\displaystyle \vec{S}=\frac{1}{\mu_0}\vec{E}\times\vec{B}\), gives energy flux and points in the direction power flows. Radiation pressure follows from momentum density \(\vec{g}=\vec{S}/c^2\), enabling solar sails and optical tweezers.

Circuits reinterpret these ideas macroscopically: inductors store magnetic energy, capacitors store electric energy, and transmission lines guide \(\vec{S}\) along conductors. Power conservation appears as Poynting’s theorem — a field-theoretic energy balance that reduces to familiar circuit relations in the right limits.

Waves, Radiation, and Antennas

Combining the curl equations yields the wave equation with speed \(\displaystyle c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}}\). Accelerating charges radiate; a short dipole emits with \(\sin^2\theta\) intensity, and the Larmor formula \(\displaystyle P=\frac{\mu_0 q^2 a^2}{6\pi c}\) gives radiated power. Antennas exploit this by driving currents that launch controlled waves across the spectrum.

Relativity and Scope

Special relativity welds \(\vec{E}\) and \(\vec{B}\) into the electromagnetic tensor \(F_{\mu\nu}\); what is purely electric in one frame can appear magnetic in another. Classical electromagnetism is the macroscopic limit of quantum electrodynamics, yet Maxwell’s equations remain the workhorse for engineering — from power grids and MRI to lasers, fiber optics, and satellites.
