Theoretical Physics

This page is a long corridor in an old library. It begins with the familiar and walks toward ideas that bend the mind. The aim is not trivia. The aim is understanding with enough depth that the symbols feel honest and the pictures feel earned. We favor clarity and structure with warmth and patience.

A theory is a promise to make sense of many facts with a few good ideas.
QParadox

What is theoretical physics

Theoretical physics is the craft of writing reality in a compressed language. The compression is not just about shorter equations. It is about fewer assumptions and cleaner principles that organize many possible experiments at once. A good theory gives you a way to ask the right questions and a way to notice when you have fooled yourself. It does not replace experiments. It points to them, shapes them, and interprets them. The engine is a cycle. You guess a structure, you translate it into mathematics, you derive consequences, you compare with measurement, you keep what survives, and you discard or refine what does not. This is not cynicism. It is respect for the world.

A useful mental model is to separate three layers: principles, models, and calculations. Principles say what kind of stories are allowed. Models pick the characters and their relations. Calculations tell you how those relations play out in time and in data. The art is to move between these layers without getting lost.

Core theories you will meet

We walk from classical to quantum with long steps and time to breathe. Each section ends with a door you can open next.

Classical

Classical mechanics: from forces to actions

Classical mechanics begins with the simple sentence that a body changes its motion when a force acts on it. The Newtonian picture turns this into $\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a}$ and then builds a toolkit for forces: springs, gravity, friction, contact. You can solve many problems with that alone, but the real depth appears when you step back and ask for a more universal viewpoint. Lagrangian mechanics says that instead of forces you should think in terms of a single scalar function $L=T-V$ and a principle of stationary action $S=\int L\,dt$. The path taken by the system is one that makes $S$ stationary with respect to small variations, which leads to the Euler–Lagrange equation $\dfrac{d}{dt}(\partial L/\partial \dot{q})-\partial L/\partial q=0$. This change of viewpoint is not just elegant. It lets you choose coordinates that match the symmetry of the problem, it makes constraints easy to handle, and it prepares your thinking for field theory and quantum mechanics. Hamiltonian mechanics goes further and rewrites the motion in terms of coordinates and conjugate momenta, with evolution generated by a single function $H$. The phase space flow preserves volume. The appearance of conserved quantities from symmetries is not a coincidence. It is the content of Noether’s theorem and is one of the deepest unifying ideas you will meet.

The shift from forces to actions is the first time you see how a new language can make old problems look obvious. The same logic will return in quantum theory, where dynamics are generated not by forces but by operators that act on states.

Fields

Electromagnetism: unity from symmetry

Electromagnetism is the first complete field theory most students encounter. Maxwell’s equations unite electricity, magnetism, and light in four compact relations. Written in differential form they say that electric charge is a source of field lines, magnetic field lines never begin or end, changing magnetic fields create electric fields, and changing electric fields create magnetic fields. The equations are consistent with special relativity and in fact help motivate it, since they pick out a universal speed $c$. The field viewpoint is not abstraction for its own sake. It explains radiation, interference, the behavior of circuits, waves in media, and the structure of atoms once quantum enters the story. When you learn to write electromagnetism in terms of a potential $A_\mu$ and a gauge symmetry, you are learning a pattern that extends to the standard model of particle physics. The idea that a redundancy in description (a gauge choice) can encode a physical long range force is one of the great surprises of modern physics and will return again when we talk about anomalies and the Higgs mechanism.

If you have ever aligned a laser and watched fringes brighten and fade, you have touched Maxwell’s equations with your hands. Theory earns its keep when it gives names and rules to what your eyes already see.

Geometry

Relativity: spacetime as a player

Special relativity is a statement about the symmetry of nature. All inertial observers see the same laws and the same speed of light. From that alone you discover that time and space mix, that simultaneity depends on motion, and that energy and mass are two faces of the same coin. General relativity moves from symmetry to geometry. Gravity is not a force that pulls over a distance. It is the curvature of spacetime produced by energy and momentum. Matter tells spacetime how to curve and curved spacetime tells matter how to move. The equations look compact $G_{\mu\nu}+ \Lambda g_{\mu\nu}=(8\pi G/c^4)T_{\mu\nu}$, but their content includes black holes, gravitational waves, the expansion of the universe, and the precise precession of Mercury. What is striking is how clean the assumptions are: local physics looks Minkowskian, motion follows geodesics, and the dynamics come from an action built from curvature scalars. Even if quantum gravity will someday modify these rules at small scales, the picture you learn here guides almost every conversation about the cosmos.

The geometric lesson is practical: if you can identify a symmetry you can choose coordinates that make the physics transparent. Geometry is the art of picking the right view.
Relativity primer

Quantum mechanics: a patient introduction

Quantum theory begins with a humble rule and a daring claim. The humble rule is that probabilities come from the squared magnitude of complex amplitudes. The daring claim is that before you record an outcome the system is not in one classical state or another. It is in a superposition that encodes possibilities and their phases. The mathematics is linear. The interpretation is subtle. We move slowly and do not mystify what can be said plainly.

States

States and observables

A quantum state is represented by a vector $|\psi\rangle$ in a Hilbert space. An observable is represented by a Hermitian operator $\hat{A}$. If you measure $\hat{A}$ on the state $|\psi\rangle$, the only outcomes you can obtain are its eigenvalues, and the probability for a particular eigenvalue is set by the projection of $|\psi\rangle$ onto the corresponding eigenspace. This sounds abstract until you work a two level system. Let $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$ be eigenstates of $\hat{\sigma}_z$. A general state is $|\psi\rangle=\alpha|0\rangle+\beta|1\rangle$ with $|\alpha|^2+|\beta|^2=1$. Measure $\hat{\sigma}_z$ and you see $+1$ with probability $|\alpha|^2$ and $-1$ with probability $|\beta|^2$. Between measurements the state evolves smoothly by the Schrödinger equation $i\hbar\,\partial_t|\psi\rangle=\hat{H}|\psi\rangle$. This split between smooth evolution and discrete outcomes is where much of the conceptual work of quantum foundations lives.

Superposition

Interference and the meaning of a phase

Interference is not exotic. It is bookkeeping for how complex amplitudes add. If there are two indistinguishable paths with amplitudes $A$ and $B$, the total amplitude is $A+B$ and the probability is $|A+B|^2$. The cross term $A^\*B+B^\*A$ depends on their relative phase. Vary the path length and the phase changes, moving probability from one detector to another. Remove which path information and fringes return. Introduce which path information and fringes fade. The lesson is that information about alternatives destroys interference, not because knowledge has a magic power but because the full state, including the environment that holds the record, changes the amplitude structure.

Limits

Uncertainty and what it really says

Heisenberg’s uncertainty relation is often misdescribed as a statement about disturbance. The clean statement is about spreads in results across many identically prepared systems. For two observables $\hat{X}$ and $\hat{P}$ with commutator $[\hat{X},\hat{P}]=i\hbar$, the standard deviations obey $\Delta X\,\Delta P\ge \hbar/2$. This is a property of the state and the operators, not your hands shaking at the apparatus. The inequality explains why there is no physical state that is a delta function in both position and momentum. It does not say you cannot know anything. It tells you what combinations of knowledge the theory allows. That is a strength. It keeps us honest about claims.

Entanglement

Entanglement and the shape of correlations

Entanglement is not mystic unity. It is the logical structure of states that cannot be written as products of parts. Consider the Bell state $(|00\rangle+|11\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$. Measure both qubits along the same axis and you see perfect correlations, yet each local outcome is random. Bell’s theorem shows that no local hidden variable model can reproduce the pattern of correlations for all chosen measurement axes. The experiments are clear. The correlations exceed the classical bound. What this rules out is a certain picture of pre existing local values. What it does not force is faster than light signaling. Quantum theory remains consistent with relativity. The puzzle is not a channel of communication. It is the structure of joint possibilities when you refuse to assign values that are not measured. When seen this way, the drama quiets and the principle comes forward: constraints on joint statistics are the heart of the matter.

Entanglement is a resource. It powers quantum teleportation, super dense coding, and forms the fuel of error correction. The same structure that surprises our intuition becomes a tool once we learn to handle it with care.
Measurement

The measurement problem and the menu of interpretations

The textbook split between unitary evolution and collapse is a rule for making predictions. It does not by itself tell a story about what the world is doing when nobody looks. Different interpretations propose different ontologies. The Copenhagen family is modest: the theory is a tool for assigning probabilities to outcomes of well defined experiments and we should not demand more. Many Worlds keeps only the unitary part and says that records of outcomes branch into non interfering histories; probabilities become branch weights. Pilot wave theory adds real particle positions guided by a wave that evolves by Schrödinger’s equation; the randomness comes from ignorance of initial conditions. Objective collapse models add a small physical mechanism that stochastically suppresses superpositions of macroscopically distinct states. Each view preserves the same empirical predictions for lab scale experiments so far, but they assign reality differently. You do not need to pick one to do physics. You do need to know that this is where the real conceptual work happens and why words like observer and measurement matter.

Foundations and less traveled roads

These topics show up in research meetings more than in textbooks. They signal depth and connect to modern frontiers.

Symmetry

Spontaneous symmetry breaking and the Higgs mechanism

A system can obey a symmetric law while choosing an asymmetric ground state. Imagine a ball balanced on top of a hill. The equations are rotationally symmetric but the resting place after a nudge points in one direction. In quantum field theory the vacuum can play the role of that resting place. When a field with a symmetric potential acquires a nonzero expectation value, the excitations around that state include massless modes (Goldstone bosons) for broken global symmetries and, if the symmetry is local (a gauge symmetry), the would be massless mode is absorbed to give mass to the gauge boson. That is the Higgs mechanism. It sounds technical because it is technical, but the picture is human. Laws can be simple while the world we inhabit is one particular choice among many allowed minima. The early universe likely passed through such transitions and left imprints we read today in particle masses and cosmic structure.

QFT

Renormalization and why infinities are not the end

Quantum field theories often produce integrals that diverge if you try to include arbitrarily high energy virtual processes. At first glance this looks fatal. The repair is not a trick. It is a recognition that the parameters you call mass and charge depend on the energy scale at which you probe the system. You introduce a cutoff to keep track of what your theory can honestly claim and then you let parameters flow with scale in a way that preserves predictions for finite quantities. The renormalization group organizes that flow and explains universality: very different microscopic systems can show the same macroscopic behavior because details are washed out by scale. Once you grasp this you stop asking a theory to tell you more than it can know. You ask it to be predictive in its range and you look for a deeper theory if new scales become relevant.

Gauge

Gauge theories and anomalies

A gauge theory begins with a redundancy in description. You can transform your fields in a way that leaves observable quantities unchanged. That redundancy demands the existence of gauge fields that act like connections, telling you how to compare phases at different points. The interactions of the standard model are built on this spine. Sometimes quantum effects spoil a symmetry that exists at the classical level. That is an anomaly. Anomalies are not small mistakes. They are warnings that a theory will not be consistent unless charges and representations are arranged to cancel the offending terms, or unless new fields appear. This is less glamorous than black holes and more decisive. A theory that mishandles anomalies is not a candidate for the world.

Geometry

Berry phase and geometric phases

Not all phases come from time evolution. If a system’s parameters are slowly cycled around a closed loop, the state can pick up a geometric phase that depends only on the path in parameter space, not on the rate. This Berry phase shows up in polarization rotation, molecular spectra, and the quantum Hall effect. It is a clue that the space of states has curvature and that physical effects can be topological, insensitive to small deformations. Once you see this, band structure in solids looks like a field theory on a lattice of momenta, with Berry curvature shaping electron motion.

Foundations

Decoherence and decoherence free subspaces

Superpositions do not vanish when nobody looks. They become entangled with their environment. When you ignore the environment the reduced state of the system looks like a classical mixture because phase relations have leaked into correlations you do not track. This is decoherence. It explains why macroscopic objects appear classical without invoking a special role for consciousness. The same mechanism suggests a strategy for quantum computing: store information in subspaces that the dominant noise channels cannot touch. These decoherence free subspaces are not a slogan. They are a design principle for real devices and a reason error correction is more than wishful thinking.

Quantum field theory: fields first, particles second

Quantum field theory treats particles as excitations of underlying fields. The vocabulary changes and so does the intuition. Interactions are encoded in the terms of a Lagrangian density. Conservation laws arise from symmetries. The vacuum is not empty but seethes with fluctuations that matter when boundaries and curvature are present.

Sum over paths

Path integrals and what “sum over histories” means

The path integral formulation says that the amplitude for a process is a sum over all possible configurations, each weighted by $e^{iS/\hbar}$ where $S$ is the action of that configuration. In simple cases this sum reduces to the stationary phase approximation you met in classical mechanics: paths near stationary action contribute coherently and dominate. In field theory this becomes an integral over field configurations. Correlation functions that you compute with Feynman diagrams are moments of that integral. The diagrams are not tiny particles bouncing off each other. They are a bookkeeping device for terms in a series expansion. The picture is powerful because it connects symmetry to selection rules and makes renormalization a natural property of how integrals respond to scale.

Vacuum

Vacuum fluctuations and the cosmological constant problem

If you let a quantum field jiggle with all its modes, the lowest energy of the vacuum seems enormous. Gravity should feel that energy as a cosmological constant, curving the universe wildly. Observationally the cosmological constant is small but nonzero. The gap between naive quantum estimates and the measured value is sometimes called the worst prediction in physics. Many ideas have been proposed to explain it, from symmetries we have not seen to environmental selection in a multiverse. None is universally accepted. The honest stance is to track exactly what is measured, to be clear about the assumptions behind estimates, and to notice where the problem might be reshaped by a better understanding of gravity in the quantum regime.

Topology

Topological phases and why order can be hidden

In ordinary phases of matter you recognize order by a local pattern: spins align, density waves form, bonds freeze. In topological phases the order is not local. It is encoded in global properties of the wavefunction, like winding numbers and Chern numbers. The quantum Hall effect and topological insulators are examples where edges carry protected modes that shrug at impurities. The protection comes from topology, not from energy barriers. This class of phases shows why phase diagrams are richer than a catalog of broken symmetries and why quantum information ideas and condensed matter ideas now share a border.

Information as a physical quantity

Information is not only a message in a notebook. It is a measurable, conserved like quantity with thermodynamic cost. Quantum mechanics turns this into a precise accounting of possibilities and correlations. The slogan that information is physical becomes literal when you count qubits and entropy.

Computation

Qubits, gates, and error correction

A qubit is any two level quantum system. Gates are unitary operations that rotate states and create entanglement. Measurement produces classical outcomes that guide later operations. Real devices are noisy so error correction is essential. The principle is to spread logical information across many physical qubits in a way that typical errors can be detected and reversed without learning the logical value itself. Threshold theorems show that if physical error rates are below a bound, arbitrarily long quantum computations are possible with enough overhead. This is an engineering challenge, not a loophole in the rules. The same mathematics that protects data in a lab protects ideas in black hole physics, where the bookkeeping of entropy and information becomes stark.

Black holes

Black hole information and holography

Black holes radiate with a thermal spectrum when quantum fields live on curved spacetime. If the radiation is purely thermal, information about the matter that formed the hole seems lost. Unitary evolution forbids that loss. The tension is the black hole information problem. A striking idea that reshapes the question is the holographic principle: the information content of a region can be described by degrees of freedom living on a lower dimensional boundary. In anti de Sitter space this becomes a duality between a quantum gravity theory in the bulk and a conformal field theory on the boundary (AdS/CFT). In that language black hole evaporation maps to a unitary process in the boundary theory, suggesting that information is not destroyed. The details are subtle and active research, but the broad lesson is clean. Geometry and quantum information are two ways of describing the same content. When ideas meet like that you are near something fundamental.

Condensed matter and the power of emergence

Many bodies obeying simple rules can produce behavior nobody would guess from a single component. This is emergence. Theoretical physics studies it with the same tools we used for fields and particles, now applied to bands, quasi particles, defects, and topology.

Quasi particles

Effective theories and why details wash out

In a crystal, electrons move in a periodic potential. Their allowed energies form bands. Near the bottom of a band the dispersion looks quadratic and you can treat excitations as free particles with an effective mass that differs from the bare electron mass. Interactions dress these carriers further into quasi particles that carry charge, spin, or heat. The marvel is that the huge complexity of $10^{23}$ electrons becomes a tractable story about a few collective modes once you identify the right variables. The renormalization group explains this washing out of microscopic detail and tells you which couplings matter at long distances. This is why a theorist can say honest things with a simple model.

Topology

Protected edges and quantized responses

In the integer quantum Hall effect the Hall conductance is quantized in units of $e^2/h$. The steps are insanely precise and do not care about the details of impurities. The reason is topological. A Chern number computed from filled bands counts something like the winding of phases across the Brillouin zone and this integer cannot change without closing a gap. The boundary then carries chiral edge modes that cannot be localized by static disorder. This is a new kind of order, and it keeps showing up in materials and in ideas borrowed by quantum information, where topology can hide information from local noise.

Cosmology: initial conditions and large scale order

The universe expands. Structures grow from tiny seeds. The microwave background glows with a near perfect blackbody spectrum and tiny anisotropies. A small set of parameters fits a vast collection of measurements. This economy is not an accident. It is the mark of a theory with few moving parts.

Early universe

Inflation and the origin of structure

Cosmic inflation is a period of accelerated expansion that stretches quantum fluctuations to astrophysical scales. These fluctuations seed the temperature variations we map in the microwave background and the later clumping into galaxies. The mechanism is model dependent but the logic is robust: a nearly constant energy density drives the expansion and small quantum jitters get blown up into classical initial conditions. The success of this picture does not make every detail settled. You still ask what field drove inflation, how it ended, and whether there are testable signatures beyond the near scale invariant spectrum we see. This is a reminder that a theory can be useful before it is final and that evidence accumulates in layers.

Dark sector

Dark matter and dark energy with a clear head

The rotation of galaxies, lensing maps, and cosmic structure argue for matter that gravitates like ordinary matter but does not emit light. This is dark matter. The accelerated expansion of the universe behaves like a small positive cosmological constant or a smooth energy component. This is dark energy. Many candidates exist for both. The careful stance is to separate what is measured from what is guessed. We see gravitational effects. We fit them with minimal extra ingredients and we search for non gravitational signals. This habit of honesty keeps speculation from turning into unearned certainty.

Quantum gravity: two languages, one world

General relativity is classical and geometric. Quantum field theory is quantum and often built on fixed spacetime backgrounds. A full theory of nature must speak both languages at once. We do not yet have that final dictionary, but there are promising dialects worth learning.

Strings

String theory and the idea of holography

Replace point particles with tiny strings and many divergences soften. Vibrational modes of the string look like the particle spectrum, including a mode that behaves like the graviton. Consistency conditions lead to extra dimensions that are compactified or otherwise hidden. The most striking development is the holographic duality in which a quantum gravity theory in a bulk spacetime is exactly equivalent to a quantum field theory living on its boundary with no gravity. This equivalence is not a metaphor. It lets you compute hard gravitational questions by translating them into gauge theory questions and returning with answers. Even if the real universe is not anti de Sitter, the conceptual lesson is large: geometry can be recast as entanglement structure.

Loops

Loop quantum gravity and the problem of time

Loop quantum gravity tries a different route. It quantizes geometry itself, leading to discrete spectra for areas and volumes. The equations that describe the state of the universe do not include an external time parameter in the usual way; the Wheeler–DeWitt equation looks like $H\Psi=0$, suggesting that time might be emergent from correlations between degrees of freedom. This “problem of time” is more than a puzzle. It forces you to examine what you mean by change and by measurement when the clock is part of the system you describe. The reward is a cleaner view of what a physical observable should be in a diffeomorphism invariant theory.

Where physics meets philosophy

Philosophy is not decoration. It is a set of tools for asking careful questions about meaning, explanation, and evidence. Physics needs that help when its own concepts reach beyond everyday intuition.

Wigner’s friend

Observers, records, and consistency

Wigner imagined his friend making a measurement inside a lab while Wigner treats the whole lab as a quantum system from outside. The friend sees a definite result. Wigner assigns a superposition. If both descriptions are valid, how do we reconcile them. One response is that quantum states are not absolute. They are relational, assigned by an observer to a system given the information available. Another response is that only decohered records accessible to both have a claim to reality. The common thread is that we should be explicit about who knows what and when, instead of using the word observer as a stand in for magic. This clarifies debates without removing the genuine puzzles that remain.

Probabilities

QBism, relational views, and superdeterminism

QBism treats the wavefunction as a personal betting book for an agent who must make decisions under uncertainty. Probabilities then live in the head, not in the world, but they are constrained by coherence rules and the structure of quantum events. Relational quantum mechanics keeps states real but relative: facts exist with respect to interactions, not in isolation. Superdeterminism points to a loophole in Bell’s theorem by denying that experimenters can choose settings independently of hidden variables. The cost is high. You must accept precise correlations that reach into choices. Thinking through these options teaches restraint. When evidence is underdetermined by theory you mark the live possibilities and you keep working.

Time

Arrow of time and Boltzmann brains

The microscopic laws of mechanics are reversible. The macroscopic world is not. The arrow of time comes from boundary conditions, not from the equations of motion. The early universe sat in a low entropy state, allowing entropy to climb. Once you accept that, you can understand why eggs break and do not reassemble and why memories point to the past. Push this line of thought far enough and you meet the Boltzmann brain problem: in a universe that lasts long enough rare fluctuations could produce isolated observers with false memories. If such observers vastly outnumber observers like us, your reasoning is in trouble. This is not an argument to stop thinking. It is a signal that cosmology must be careful about measures and about which ensembles are meaningful.

How to keep going

Read actively. Derive small results yourself. Draw diagrams. Keep a notebook of questions and return to them after each section. Understanding is slow and cumulative. The reward is that once you learn to think this way you can approach any new topic without fear.

Practice

Core skills to drill

Get fluent with linear algebra identities and Dirac notation. Learn to move between Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics. Practice dimensional analysis until it feels like a reflex. Work basic variational problems and simple eigenvalue problems by hand. Write short codes that produce the plots you wish your mind could see. These are not chores. They are the grammar of the subject, and mastery of grammar is the path to style.

Experiments

Bring the lab into the story

Theory earns its living when it helps design a measurement or interpret a dataset. Read about interferometers, spectrometers, cryogenic tricks, and noise budgets. Learn how calibration and error bars turn a beautiful idea into a believable claim. When you can sketch a setup and predict its limitations, you have crossed from reading to doing.
