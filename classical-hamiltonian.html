Classical Mechanics — Hamiltonian

From energy functions to phase space: the Hamiltonian is the gateway from classical mechanics to modern physics.

The Hamiltonian Idea

The Hamiltonian formulation emerged as the next step in the evolution of mechanics. While Lagrange expressed dynamics in terms of coordinates and velocities, William Rowan Hamilton discovered a way to reformulate the laws of motion using coordinates and their conjugate momenta. The Hamiltonian function \(H\) often represents the total energy of the system: \(H = T + V\). But more than just energy, it is a function that generates the equations of motion. Instead of second-order equations, Hamiltonian mechanics gives first-order equations for positions and momenta, and opens the door to phase space, symplectic geometry, and quantum mechanics.

Hamilton’s Equations

Starting from the Lagrangian, the momentum conjugate to coordinate \(q_i\) is defined as \[ p_i = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q_i}. \] The Hamiltonian is obtained by a Legendre transform: \[ H(q_i,p_i,t) = \sum_i p_i \dot q_i - L(q_i,\dot q_i,t). \] The dynamics are then given by Hamilton’s equations: \[ \dot q_i = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \qquad \dot p_i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}. \] These compact relations encode the entire motion of the system. Unlike Newton’s forces or Lagrange’s equations, Hamilton’s equations emphasize symmetry between coordinates and momenta, and they generalize naturally into advanced physics.

Phase Space

Phase space is the multidimensional arena where Hamiltonian mechanics unfolds. Each state of a system is represented by a point with coordinates \((q_i, p_i)\). As time evolves, the state traces a trajectory in this space. A simple harmonic oscillator appears as an ellipse in phase space. A pendulum, for small oscillations, shows closed orbits; at larger energies, its trajectory spirals toward continuous rotation. Unlike configuration space alone, phase space preserves all information about the system, making it the natural habitat for dynamics. Liouville’s theorem guarantees that volumes in phase space are preserved under Hamiltonian flow, a profound statement about the conservation of information in classical physics.

Example: Harmonic Oscillator

For a one-dimensional oscillator with mass \(m\) and spring constant \(k\), \[ H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}kx^2. \] Hamilton’s equations are \(\dot x = p/m, \quad \dot p = -kx\). Together they form a system of first-order equations whose solution describes sinusoidal motion. In phase space, the trajectory is an ellipse, with energy corresponding to the size of the orbit. This picture is richer than the simple sinusoidal solution: it shows the complete state at every instant. The oscillator becomes a prototype system whose Hamiltonian structure echoes in quantum mechanics, where energy levels are quantized.

Symmetry and Conservation

One of the most elegant aspects of the Hamiltonian approach is its direct connection to symmetry. In phase space, translations in a coordinate lead to conservation of momentum, while invariance under time shifts guarantees conservation of energy. Canonical transformations — changes of coordinates in phase space that preserve Hamilton’s structure — reveal the deep geometry underlying mechanics. This framework is the stage on which Emmy Noether’s theorem shines, showing that every continuous symmetry corresponds to a conserved quantity. The Hamiltonian is thus not just a tool for solving problems but a window into the architecture of the laws of physics.

Beyond Classical Mechanics

The Hamiltonian formalism extends far beyond simple mechanics. In statistical mechanics, the Hamiltonian defines the energy function used to compute partition functions and thermodynamic quantities. In quantum mechanics, the Hamiltonian operator governs time evolution through Schrödinger’s equation: \[ i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\Psi = \hat H \Psi. \] In quantum field theory, the Hamiltonian density determines the dynamics of fields and particles. Even in general relativity, Hamiltonian methods provide tools for analyzing spacetime evolution. What began as a rephrasing of Newton’s laws becomes the universal bridge between classical and modern physics.
