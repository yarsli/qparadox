Classical Mechanics — Hamiltonian

Hamilton’s mechanics reframes motion as flows of energy and momentum through phase space, shaping the path from classical to quantum physics.

Historical Background

William Rowan Hamilton (1805–1865) was both a mathematician and physicist, a figure who saw beauty in unifying algebra and geometry. Dissatisfied with the limitations of Newton’s forces and even Lagrange’s actions, Hamilton envisioned a formalism where mechanics would mirror the structure of optics. His equations, first written in the 1830s, did not simply solve old problems: they opened entirely new ones, paving the way for modern theories of energy, probability, and symmetry.

Phase Space and Energy Landscapes

Phase space is the stage of Hamiltonian mechanics. Each point in this abstract space represents a complete state of the system: all coordinates \(q_i\) and all conjugate momenta \(p_i\). A pendulum, for instance, needs only two variables, angle \(\theta\) and angular momentum \(p_\theta\). A planet requires six: three positions and three momenta. In this space, motion becomes a trajectory that never crosses itself, threading smoothly through an energy landscape defined by the Hamiltonian.

The Hamiltonian Function

The Hamiltonian is generally written as \[ H(q,p,t) = T(p) + V(q,t). \] This is deceptively simple: kinetic plus potential energy. Yet the real power lies in how Hamilton used it. From this single function, one can derive all equations of motion, identify conserved quantities, and analyze stability. Unlike the Lagrangian, which is tied to velocities, the Hamiltonian speaks the language of momenta — the very concept later quantized in wave mechanics.

Hamilton’s Equations

Hamilton’s equations are a pair of symmetric differential equations: \[ \dot{q}_i = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \qquad \dot{p}_i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}. \] They replace Newton’s \(\vec{F}=m\vec{a}\) with rules that treat position and momentum as equal partners. In this framework, time evolution is not a force pushing matter forward, but the unfolding of geometry in phase space.

Illustrative Examples

Free particle: \( H = \tfrac{p^2}{2m} \). Position increases linearly; momentum is constant.

Harmonic oscillator: \( H = \tfrac{p^2}{2m} + \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 q^2 \). Motion is sinusoidal; phase space shows ellipses of constant energy.

Pendulum: \( H(\theta,p_\theta) = \tfrac{p_\theta^2}{2ml^2} + mgl(1-\cos\theta) \). Trajectories differ: oscillations at low energy, rotations at high energy.

Planetary orbits: In central potentials, the Hamiltonian encodes the Kepler problem, and conserved angular momentum naturally emerges from the equations.

From Lagrangian to Hamiltonian

The bridge between formalisms is the Legendre transform: \[ H(q,p) = \sum_i p_i \dot{q}_i - L(q,\dot{q}). \] This transformation swaps velocities for momenta. While the Lagrangian highlights principles of least action, the Hamiltonian highlights the conservation and flow of energy. The change is not cosmetic — it allows mechanics to merge seamlessly with the operator methods of quantum physics.

Canonical Transformations

Canonical transformations are changes of variables in phase space that preserve the form of Hamilton’s equations. They act like rotations and shifts in this abstract landscape, leaving the physics untouched but often simplifying the mathematics. Action-angle coordinates, for example, can turn complicated periodic motion into simple uniform rotation in a new set of variables.

Poisson Brackets

The Poisson bracket is defined as \[ \{f,g\} = \sum_i \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial q_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q_i} \right). \] It measures how two quantities evolve relative to each other in phase space. The time evolution of any function \(f(q,p)\) can be written as \(\dot{f} = \{f,H\} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}\). In quantum mechanics, Poisson brackets are replaced by commutators, making them the classical ancestor of quantum algebra.

Liouville’s Theorem

Liouville’s theorem states that as a system evolves under Hamilton’s equations, the volume of any region of phase space remains constant. In other words, Hamiltonian flow is incompressible. This principle underpins statistical mechanics: probability distributions stretch and twist but never shrink or grow. The idea that information is preserved in motion resonates deeply with thermodynamics, entropy, and even black hole physics.

Modern Significance

Hamiltonian mechanics is no relic of the 19th century. In quantum theory, the Hamiltonian operator defines energy levels and governs time evolution. In relativity, Hamiltonian constraints describe spacetime itself. In chaos theory, Hamiltonian systems display sensitive dependence on initial conditions, yet still preserve volume. Every corner of modern physics — from condensed matter to cosmology — carries Hamilton’s imprint.
